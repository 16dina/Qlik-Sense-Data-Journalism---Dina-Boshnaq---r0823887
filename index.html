Introduction

According to Inside Airbnb's website, they made a statement saying “Airbnb claims to be part of the "sharing economy" and disrupting the hotel industry. However, data shows that the majority of Airbnb listings in most cities are entire homes, many of which are rented all year round - disrupting housing and communities.”

The only people who could actually regulate this phenomenon and make sure that the Airbnb industry isn't disrupting residential areas, are policymakers. But like any other legislation, they need compelling evidence to show the need for rules and limitations on these types of short-term accommodations. Let's look at the data to get a better understanding of the situation.

We will be looking at data from 3 big cities in Belgium: Ghent, Brussels, and Antwerp. Overall, the number of Airbnbs in these cities is 9498, Brussels being the city with the most Airbnbs according to the following heatmap. This is expected since it's the capital of Belgium. And of course, each city has neighbourhoods. If you click on a city you can get an overview of the neighbourhoods ordered by number of Airbnbs. This is just to give you an understanding of the landscape of Airbnbs.

But now, let's focus more on the problem at hand, are Airbnbs affecting the house market and disrupting local residence? The first step is to check whether these Airbnbs are entire houses or not. Click on a neighbourhood to get a first glimpse of the distribution of different types of Airbnbs within it. Most likely you'll find that entire houses have the biggest share. But perhaps you clicked on one that's not like that, so let's zoom out and look at the percentages for each Airbnb type in each city as a whole.
