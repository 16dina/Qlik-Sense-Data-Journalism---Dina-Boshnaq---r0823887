Property Ownership & Types of Hosts

One key aspect to look into is the market share analysis, which provides insights into the concentration of Airbnb listings among different hosts. By examining the number of hosts with multiple listings, we can better understand whether a small number of individuals or owners dominate the market.

Overall, the number of hosts that own only 1 property are the highest being 4720 hosts out of 5907. 652 hosts have 2 properties, 238 hosts have 3 properties, and 302 hosts have more than 3 properties.

Relatively speaking the number of hosts owning more than 3 properties might sound little, but 302 people is still a big number. The total number of Airbnbs is 9498, 4720 people own 1 Airbnb each, that's 4720 Airbnbs which is about half of the total. The remaining 1187 hosts own the other half! If we do the math 1187/5907 is approximately 0.2 which is a 1/5. A fifth of the total hosts own half the Airbnb market! Go ahead and click on the "more" bar in the bar chart (You will notice the bar chart expands to show the details of "more" like 4, 5, 6 properties, feel free to explore the numbers on your own). We conclude that 2755 Airbnbs are owned by 302 people.

If we look at the maximum number of properties managed by one host, we find that 119 Airbnbs are managed by a host called Luca. He effectively owns 1.25% of the Airbnb market in the 3 cities. That's a lot considering there's more than five thousand hosts. 2nd place is an establishment/hotel called Sweet Inn with 116 properties and 3rd place is a host called Elisa with 97 properties. 2 of the 3 top rankers are individual people.

As you may have already assumed, most of the hosts are single individuals. But there are also group owners, like families or partners. Their numbers are not high though, see the bar chart below.

There are also Airbnbs listed as part of hotels or establishments, but those are way less in number, barely amounting to around 21 more or less.

Host Verification

Usually in Airbnbs, the hosts need to be verified in order to ensure a level of trust and accountability which are crucial factors in maintaining the integrity of short-term accommodations. Like any other system, some people break that rule and don't get verified. It is important for policymakers to know whether or not these hosts are legitimate in order to address any legal issues that arise from the prevalence of Airbnbs. Policymakers must prioritize host verification to enforce legal compliance, address potential issues, and promote quality control in short-term accommodations. This process not only fosters a secure and safe environment for guests but also establishes a foundation for harmonious coexistence between Airbnb hosts and the local community. But unfortunately, there seems to be 584 hosts who are unverified, this makes up ~10% of hosts in the three cities being unverified.

So not only are most Airbnbs entire houses, but in some cases their hosts might not be verified, and the majority of those hosts are individual people. Another important thing to look into is whether these Airbnbs are used for commercial-use only or just as a side business for some. Perhaps these houses are only used as Airbnbs occasionally, and not year round or for long. Let's see what the data we have says about this.
